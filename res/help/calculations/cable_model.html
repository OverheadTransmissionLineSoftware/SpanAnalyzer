2. Cable Model

2.1 Overview

The cable model describes how the cable elongates as load increases. The x-axis represents strain, while the y-axis represents load.

The cable elongation models are similar to material stress-strain models, but have some important differences.

  • The modulus of elasticity (stiffness) cannot be determined by material properties alone. The stranding design has a significant impact on the effective stiffness. This is difficult to account for analytically, and is often determined by empirical tests.
  • For a new cable, plastic elongation occurs after any amount of load is applied. There isn't a specific yield stress as there is in material stress-strain models. When the cable is unloaded, the cable follows a different modulus of elasticity that accounts for the plastic elongation.

2.2 Initial Polynomial

Elongation is modeled using a 4th order polynomial. To create the polynomial, data points are collected in a lab test and are regression fitted. The polynomial represents a new cable that has been subjected to minimal stretching. This state is called the 'Initial' condition.




Depending on how the regression fitting was done, the polynomial may pass through the origin, or it may intersect the x-axis at an arbitrary point. Although it is ideal that zero load equals zero strain, this is not necessary. The polynomial slope also typically decreases, indicating that that more plastic strain occurs at high loads.

2.4 Stretch

Stretch is modeled two separate ways. The methods are not additive - either the cable stretches from one or the other. Both methods rely on a combination of tension and time exposure to set a stretch point on the Initial Polynomial.



If the load is decreased relative to the stretch point, the cable follows a different stiffness modulus than the polynomial. This stretched modulus, commonly referred to as the 'Final' modulus, is constant and should be steeper than any point on the Initial Polynomial.

If the load is increased relative to the stretch point, the cable follows the Initial Polynomial. The stretch point will shift to match the highest load along the polynomial.

2.4.1 Creep Stretch Point

This method of stretching is caused at relatively low tensions over a long period of time. A special Creep Polynomial is used that shows how much plastic elongation has occurred relative to the Initial Polynomial. Like the Initial Polynomial, the Creep Polynomial uses data points that are collected in a long term lab test. The data points are typically extrapolated out to 10 years, and then are regression fitted to define the polynomial.

To find the stretch point, a point on the Creep Polynomial is identified that matches the everyday loading of the cable. The Final modulus is extended from the Creep Polynomial point until it intersects the Initial Polynomial, which will set the stretch point.



2.4.2 Load Stretch Point

This method of stretching is caused at relatively high tensions over a short period of time. Icing and wind is typically associated with load based stretching. The highest load experienced by the cable sets the stretch point on the Initial polynomial.



2.5 Components

Cables can be composed of non-homogenous materials. For high tension cables, there is often a core component which mechanically supports the outer shell component which conducts electricity. To model non-homogenous cables, two sets of data points are needed: one for the outer shell and one for the core. The total cable model can be determined by summing the core and shell components.




Solving for the shell data points allows the component polynomial, temperature, and stretch to be modeled independently and summed together to model the load-strain behavior of the entire cable.
